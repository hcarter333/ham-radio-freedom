Coaxial Cable

 

 

Coaxial cable is commonly used to carry radio frequency signals between a radio and an antenna.  A coaxial cable is shown above.  It consists of:

A.  protective jacket

B.  shielding conductor

C.  dielectric insulator

D.  center conductor

 

The most common failure of coaxial cable is a break in the protective jacket leading to moisture entering the cable.  Coaxial cable with a jacket that is resistive to ultraviolet radiation will last longer in outdoor applications where the protective jacket is exposed to ultraviolet light from the sun.  One disadvantage of air core coaxial cable is that it requires special preparation to avoid moisture contamination.

 

Impedance is a measure of the resistance the cable presents to the flow of current.  Or you could say it’s a measure of how much the cable impedes the flow of current.  The most commonly used coaxial cable for amateur radio has  an impedance of 50 ohms.  This works out well for a number of reasons.  For example, a center fed dipole antenna has an impedance of 50 ohms.

 

Some of the radio frequency signal is lost in the coaxial cable.  For a given cable, the higher the frequency of the transmitted signal, the more higher the signal loss.  In general, at a given frequency, the larger the diameter of the cable, the less signal will be lost.
