Antenna Types

There are many types of antennas used by amateur radio stations.  Antenna characteristics are selected based on the needs of each station.  Some antennas are better for small spaces, others are better for beaming as much radio frequency energy as possible in one directions and others are very easy to build and get on the air quickly.  For the technician class amateur radio exam, you’ll need to know about the following antenna types and terms:

Beam Antenna:  Beam antenna is another term used to describe a directional antenna.  There are several types of beam antennas, but they all serve one basic purpose: to concentrate transmitted RF energy in a single direction, and to eliminate interference from received stations that aren’t located in that same direction.  The increase of signal strength  in that single direction compared to an antenna that radiates equally in every direction is called the antenna’s gain.

Yagi Antenna:  A yagi antenna for the two meter band is shown above.  Of the three crossbars, (called elements), the middle one is driven with RF energy, the one at the bottom is called a reflector and the element at the top is called a director.

Quad Antenna: The quad antenna, (shown above), is a directional antenna as well.  It was invented by Clarence C. Moore, W9LZX.

Dish Antenna:  Dish antennas are directional antennas that are familiar to most people as satellite tv antennas.  They are also used for amateur radio transmission and reception.  The picture above shows the largest dish antenna in the world at the Arecibo observatory.  It is 1000 feet across.

Rubber Duck Antenna:  The rubber duck antenna is popular for handheld radios.  While it is not as effective as full sized antennas and its signal strength may be reduced even more when used inside a vehicle, its small size makes it easy to use and carry for portable operations.

Dipole Antenna:  The dipole antenna is one of the simplest antennas to deploy.  It consists of a half wavelength of wire split into two quarter wavelength pieces and suspended from a piece of feeder line as shown in the figure above.  The radiation from this antenna is strongest in the two directions perpendicular, or broadside to the wire.
