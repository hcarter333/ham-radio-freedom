Capacitors

A capacitor is an electrical component that is composed of two or more conductors separated by insulators.  A capacitor will not conduct DC currents.  It will conduct AC currents.  When a DC current is applied to a capacitor, the capacitor will pass a small amount of the current initially, but it will ultimately allow none of the current to pass.  An ohmmeter attached across a capacitor will exhibit this phenomenon by reading a smaller resistance at first, followed by readings that increase with time.

 

Capacitors store energy in an electric field.  This energy can be discharged if the capacitor has a path to ground.  Care must be taken when working on circuitry with large capacitors, (such as televisions), to make sure that the capacitors are discharged.  Otherwise, by touching the capacitor, you can become a path to ground and receive a dangerous electric shock.
